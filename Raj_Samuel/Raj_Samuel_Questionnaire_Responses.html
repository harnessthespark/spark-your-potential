1 AMV/BBDO & Brands Transformation
AMV/BBDO was the biggest agency in the UK - but they were getting systematically locked out of digital pitches. Clients saw them as TV craft specialists. Great at TV. Shit at digital. BT Digital was one of the rare pitches they even got invited to. They brought me in as a freelance planning director specifically for that pitch. We won it. And then the real work started - because we'd promised digital capabilities we didn't actually have yet. We had to build them while delivering. What agency leadership couldn't see at first was that the real threat wasn't "we don't have digital departments" - it was that we'd stopped living in culture while everyone else was already there. I naturally saw this - the fix wasn't hiring digital specialists and calling it a day. It was teaching 450 people to think culturally first, with technology as just another tool for connection. After the BT win, I became Board Account Planner, then Co-Innovation Director. Spent five years building what we'd promised: new departments, new ways of working, shifting our pitch pipeline from 90% TV to 60% integrated/digital. Basically repositioning how the entire agency operated. We proved it worked through the actual client work. BT evolved from telco to tech brand - 2M+ people engaged. Aviva went from "insurance company" to "life enabler" - 2M visits across 87 countries. Mercedes shifted from pure TV to digitally-enhanced experiences that people actually wanted to participate in. Plus award-winning work for The Economist, Cancer Research UK, Famous Grouse. Each campaign proved the same thing: when you understand culture deeply, technology stops being a separate problem. It just becomes part of how you connect.
2 Cadbury Gorilla Digital (and launch)
Fallon brought me in to do the digital strategy for Cadbury. The creative team had come up with this absolutely insane idea - a drumming gorilla. No chocolate. No bite. Just a gorilla, Phil Collins' "In the Air Tonight," and pure joy. The client was terrified. People at Cadbury genuinely thought they'd lose their jobs if they ran it - the brand had NEVER done an ad without someone taking a bite of chocolate. Never. Part of my digital strategy work was running workshops to help them understand what this could mean. They kept saying this would make Cadbury a "content company" - which sounded adventurous but they didn't actually know what that meant. So I reframed it. I told them: this ad isn't the content strategy. It's an invitation. It's a promise that says "we have more fun like this coming" - and people will come searching for it. THAT'S what being in content means. The workshop showed them how content actually lives in the world now - big brands, random kids online, everyone remixing everything. We had to figure out how to make people share what little we had - which was basically just one ad. So the digital strategy included building an online destination. Finding remixers online and getting them to remix the Gorilla with other 80s tracks - made it look like random people were doing it organically, not us. The real clincher? The workshop created belief. Showed them proof of what could happen. Gave them the courage to actually run the thing. And then yeah, the ad became one of the most famous campaigns of the 2000s. The creative was brilliant - but it was the digital strategy and that workshop that gave them permission to be brave enough to greenlight it in the first place. I kept working on Cadbury's digital strategy beyond Gorilla - helping them become an actual content brand, not just a chocolate company that did one viral ad.
3 Arley's Brand Creation
2020-2022. First time going client-side. Joined Arley's as Brand Director - plant-based food startup, crowded market, needed to build the entire brand from zero. What changed was taking a generic "plant-based startup" idea and transforming it into a "heritage comfort brand" that secured listings with three major retailers in 12 months and attracted ¬£1.2M in pre-launch revenue. What the founders couldn't see - and what I uncovered through research across five markets - was the "comfort paradox": 73% of flexitarian consumers actually wanted familiarity over innovation. Everyone in the category was chasing "the future" when the real opportunity was "the familiar, but better." What came naturally was doing the cultural research that revealed this counterintuitive insight, then translating it into a brand story that investors and retailers immediately understood. We weren't selling disruption - we were selling comfort you could feel good about. Startup life meant wearing multiple hats. I was designing investment decks, crafting the story for funding rounds, getting involved in naming conversations, packaging decisions - all of it. We worked with Mother, one of the most famous agencies in the UK, on the creative. The name ultimately became the name of the main founder's house - Arley's - which felt very British and homegrown. The logic worked. Mother came up with a design that felt properly British. Heritage. Real. The positioning felt obvious in hindsight, but only because I'd made the invisible pattern visible. Brand's still going strong - now have their own factory, selling in retailers across Europe.
4 Malice Arts - Immersive Events
2014-2019. I'd always been fascinated by live events. Did club nights for years, kept evolving them to be more experiential - 3D glasses, making entire rooms fall in love, that kind of thing. Eventually realized I had to actually invest and make them full productions. First one was Malice in Wonderland - took the original story and turned it into a horror experience. The Malice name stuck. The idea was always about adding darkness to existing themes, twisting things people knew into something new. Bit brave taking existing IP and creating these underground events, but I got away with it. Sometimes did originals like 1984 vs 1984 (Orwell meets MTV) or Inferno/Paradiso (Heaven & Hell). But mostly twisted pop culture: Tron characters crash-landing in a club, Narnia glitching inside the Matrix, climate change hitting a surreal winterland. Think it eventually became a learning lab for me - and I just kept going. Not just 3 or 4 events. Ten. 5,000+ guests across the series. 85% return rates. Secured brand sponsorships. Genuine press coverage. What I proved was that when you create worlds people want to enter - not just events they attend - they come back and bring friends. Audiences don't just want to be entertained, they want to be transported. They're hungry for experiences that respect their intelligence and give them agency within a crafted narrative. What came naturally was architecting these multi-sensory worlds where every element - music selection, spatial design, performer interactions, even the invitation sequence - built toward a cohesive emotional journey. Looking back, I was testing everything I believed about how culture and experience move people. And the metrics proved it worked.
5 Lemons.life ‚Üí Quilter Partnership
Met Alex. Her husband had died young - suddenly faced with sorting out wills, financial planning, all of it while grieving. The system was broken, especially for women. We decided to co-found something to fix it. I came up with the naming and brand strategy. Here's the thing - we could've just come up with a "wills" name. Keep it narrow. But I saw it bigger. We were originally just going to drive leads to someone else to sort out the actual wills. Standard referral model. But I went and found a wills company that does white labelling. Game changer. Because now we could own the customer data - not the wills info itself, just the marketing data. This was our saving grace. Now we had a reason to build a real brand and own the customer relationship. So instead of "WillsForWomen" or whatever, we went with Lemons. "Life will always throw Lemons." Not just for wills. Any financial planning challenge. Preparing for future lemons. The brand had room to grow. What I noticed early - and what others initially missed - was that the real story wasn't about death at all. It was about women's financial vulnerability. The massive gaps in planning for life transitions. Career breaks. Caring responsibilities. Divorce. Widowhood. All the lemons life throws at women specifically. The Quilter partnership came through our wills provider - who's now a shareholder. And now the mission feels complete. Quilter's investing. They're providing female financial advisers to sell. Women selling to women. Turning the world of finance upside down for women. ISAs, pensions, life cover, the works. What came naturally was taking a taboo topic (death) and reframing it through a culturally relevant lens (women's financial security) that felt urgent and actionable rather than morbid. The strategic positioning made the difference between being a niche will-writing service and becoming a platform that could genuinely expand financial security for our target audience. Five years later, we're about to actually do it.
